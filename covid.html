Introduction Rationale Tweets Limitations Key Concepts

Introduction

 Covid-19 has developed into a global pandemic since the beginning of 2020. From the moment that the first case was found in the U.S. on January 20, the U.S. passed 200,000 deaths on September 22 (Chappell). On September 29, 7 days after the record-breaking number of deaths, both candidates participated in the discussion about the virus in the first presidential debate.
 Biden argued that “the president has no plan” to deal with the growing number of cases in the U.S., while Trump argued back that he is “doing a phenomenal job” against the virus.


 What we care about here is people’s reaction towards both candidates’ speech and to evaluate Twitter as a platform of recording the historical events for future use.

Rationale for Searching Tweets

 To investigate people’s reaction towards the discussion regarding Covid-19 in the first presidential debate, we used #debate2020 and #election2020 as hashtags. To filter out the tweets that are more relevant to Covid among millions of tweets that contain these hashtags, we used the advanced search function in Twitter. In the “Any of these words” session of the advanced search, we put “#debate2020 #election2020 Covid”, which provides us the tweets that contain any combination of these three keywords: it could be any one of them, any two of them or all of them. To obtain more influential results, we added a restriction that the tweets had to have more than 1,000 likes. Finally, since the first presidential debate was on September 29 and we observed a trend that real-time reactions usually grabbed more attention, we focused on the tweets that were posted from September 29, 2020 to October 3, 2020.

Limitations of Twitter

 With the acknowledgement of the need for a more massive dataset and a more professional analyzing tool than human analysts, we noticed that Twitter users receive biased feeds based on their political opinions. If you are a supporter for one of the candidates or parties, it is harder to reach tweets that are in favor of the other. This results in consolidating people’s thoughts even more and it would be harder for anyone who wants to stay neutral or objective. It creates a world that you have to pick a side and put your root in it. However, this is an impact and it is not necessary to categorize it as a benefit or a limitation: it is beneficial for people as it is more convenient for them to obtain the information that they need, while it is harmful as it may block people’s sight at the same time.
 However, we did notice a limitation of Twitter’s search engine when we used it to gather information: there were not just two perspectives regarding this topic and the search results blocked almost all the perspectives other than democrats (Biden) and republicans (Trump). Both the presidential debate and the Covid-19 issue are global topics. Not only Americans were paying attention to them and discussing them. The hashtags #debate2020 and #election2020 were the most popular ones, but they rarely included any other tweets except those that were posted by Americans. It is true that there is a filter for languages in the search engine, but the amount of data is significantly low for studying. Except for the reason that fewer people outside the US reacted to these topics, there is also another possibility that people who are native in other languages do not use an English hashtag. Even though they may have a discussion in another hashtag that is worth to be included in the study, it is almost impossible for the mass public to know and understand their opinions.

Key Concepts from Class Readings

 The way we search the tweets and try to understand the presidential debate as a historical event via social media (i.e. Twitter) does show how valuable the social media is as an ethical use and preservation of data (Galarza 792). Although it is a large database sufficient for research use, finding an efficient way to filter out information remains an issue. A lot of Twitter users’ tweets are too short or empty to provide any useful information. The brevity of tweets “encourages the kind of cryptic, condensed language associated with text messaging in general.” (Arceneanux 1268) The misunderstanding created by the brevity leads to unnecessary arguments/data. Therefore, finding useful data among people’s chatting tweets in such a huge database is definitely challenging and obscured.
